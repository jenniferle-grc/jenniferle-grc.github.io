Clarendon vs. Sutro

History of each typeface

Clarendon

Clarendon is a slab serif typeface originally designed by Robert Besley and released by Thorogood & Co. at the Fann Street Foundry in London in 1845. It was one of the first registered typefaces and was created during a time when bold slab serifs were becoming popular for posters, advertisements, and signage. Named after the Clarendon Press in Oxford, it was designed to be bold, readable, and attention-grabbing while still working well for body text. A variation called French Clarendon later became popular in the United States and became closely associated with American Old West wanted posters. Today, Clarendon is still widely used for branding, signage, and editorial design because of its timeless and highly readable letterforms.

Sutro

Sutro is a slab serif typeface designed by Jim Parkinson and released in 2003 through Parkinson Type Design in Oakland, California. Parkinson created Sutro after decades of studying nineteenth-century square serif and wood type, inspired by designer Rob Roy Kelly's wood type collection and his own redesign of the Egiziano typeface in the 1970s. Named after Adolph Sutro, the former mayor of San Francisco, the typeface reflects the bold Victorian lettering found around the historic Sutro Baths. Sutro includes a large family of weights and decorative styles, including shaded, hatched, and layered versions, making it useful for both display typography and body text while giving designers many creative options.
